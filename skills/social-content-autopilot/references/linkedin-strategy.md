# LinkedIn organic-reach strategy

Reviewed 2026-06-20. Use with `formats/linkedin.md` and
`references/linkedin-lunatics.md`.

## Evidence and limits

- LinkedIn's [feed-ranking explanation](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a9554004)
  says ranking uses hundreds of signals from post context, profile, network, and
  activity. Virality therefore depends on audience relevance and network fit,
  not only copy structure.
- LinkedIn's [recommendation guidance](https://www.linkedin.com/blog/content-recommendations-avoid)
  says helpful professional insights and constructive conversations are more
  suitable for wider recommendation. Pure promotion, direct engagement bait,
  pods, irrelevant tags, unoriginal posts, and unconstructive provocation may
  not travel beyond direct connections.
- LinkedIn's [organic analytics](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a701208)
  expose in-network and out-of-network impressions, reach, reactions, comments,
  reposts, saves, and sends. Use those fields to test distribution hypotheses.
- LinkedIn's [AI-content guidance](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a1481496)
  makes the member responsible for review and approval. Retain human facts,
  judgment, and voice instead of publishing generic AI prose.
- LinkedIn's [content guidance](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a417973)
  emphasizes relevant, authentic, concise hooks and professional value such as
  advice, education, and interesting facts. Sponsored-content observations are
  hypotheses for organic creative, not organic ranking guarantees.

## Virality model

Define useful LinkedIn virality as out-of-network distribution among the right
professional audience followed by saves, sends, substantive discussion, profile
visits, follows, or business actions.

Build for four gates:

1. **Professional relevance:** the target role or industry recognizes the issue.
2. **Credible tension:** a true decision, result, or opinion creates curiosity.
3. **Transferable value:** the reader learns a method, tradeoff, or mental model.
4. **Conversation:** qualified people can add evidence, disagreement, or nuance.

The high-drama style improves presentation only. It cannot compensate for weak
expertise, an irrelevant network, or unsupported claims.

## Content archetypes

| Archetype | Hook mechanism | Body payload | Desired action |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| contrarian lesson | challenge accepted practice | evidence + boundary conditions | qualified debate |
| build decision | cost, rejection, or reversal | tradeoff + decision rule | saves/comments |
| failure analysis | surprising mistake | cause + corrective framework | sends/saves |
| mini case study | concrete outcome | starting state + intervention + result | profile/lead action |
| operator framework | recurring problem | named steps + example | saves |
| industry reaction | timely development | original implication | comments/reposts |
| product lesson | user problem | insight first, product mechanism second | qualified visits |
| artifact teardown | visible example | annotations + recommendations | saves/sends |

Reject an archetype when the source context lacks its proof.

## Hook engineering

Use one tension mechanism and pay it off quickly:

- a decision that looked wrong but produced a verified result
- a commonly repeated rule with an important exception
- a costly mistake and the diagnostic that would have caught it
- a concrete result paired with the non-obvious cause
- two legitimate professional values in conflict
- an industry event with a second-order implication

Avoid a hook that relies on missing context, exaggerates certainty, or changes
the meaning of the source. Put a proof marker—number, artifact, observation, or
specific event—near the opening.

## Body and debate design

- Use 3-6 staccato high-drama beats, then increase information density.
- Make the professional lesson usable without buying the product.
- State boundary conditions: when the advice does and does not apply.
- Prefer one memorable model to many shallow tips.
- Ask a narrow question that a practitioner can answer from experience.
- Invite disagreement with the premise, tradeoff, or implementation—not a
  generic reaction.

## Distribution context

Before drafting, inspect whether the author profile supports the claimed topic,
whether the intended audience exists in the network, and whether visibility
allows broader reach. Recommend profile or network positioning work when those
inputs are the likely bottleneck. Copy optimization alone does not create topic
authority or relevant connections.

After publishing, respond to substantive comments with new information and
clarifying questions. Do not use canned replies or manufacture comment chains.

## Measurement

Compare with the median of at least 10 comparable posts by format and topic:

- out-of-network impressions / total impressions
- saves + sends + reposts / impressions
- substantive comments / impressions
- profile visits / members reached
- follows / profile visits
- qualified link visits or conversions

Classify comments as substantive only when they add experience, reasoning,
evidence, or a real question. Do not count generic praise as proof of topic fit.

## Controlled tests

- confession vs. contrarian hook
- drama dial 2 vs. 4
- story-first vs. framework-first
- text vs. evidence image/document
- debate prompt vs. no question
- broad industry framing vs. role-specific framing
- insight-only vs. insight plus product connection

Do not publish near-duplicates at the same time. Change one variable, record the
hypothesis, and use several observations before adopting a rule.
